### The Complete Step-by-Step Path From "Never Used Claude" to "Works With Claude Every Day"

This guide is long on purpose. It's built so you can follow it start to finish, or jump to whatever Part matches where you're stuck right now. Every Part is broken into Steps. ## Part 1: What Claude Actually Is (5 minutes)

### Step 1.1 — Understand what you're working with
- 1.1.a Claude is an AI assistant made by a company called Anthropic. You talk to it by typing (or speaking), and it responds in real, readable language — not robotic computer code.
- 1.1.b Claude does not "know" you between separate conversations unless you use a feature called **Projects** (covered in Part 3). Each new chat starts fresh unless you tell it otherwise.
- 1.1.c Claude can read documents you upload, write documents for you, analyze data, write and run code, and hold a back-and-forth conversation like a very well-read colleague.

### Step 1.2 — Know what Claude is NOT
- 1.2.a It is not a search engine. It doesn't "look things up" on the live internet by default in every mode — it reasons from what it was trained on, plus anything you give it directly in the conversation.
- 1.2.b It is not always right. Treat it like a very capable junior colleague: smart, fast, occasionally confidently wrong. Always sanity-check anything important (numbers, legal language, medical claims).
- 1.2.c It does not remember your previous separate conversations by default. If continuity matters, use Projects (Part 3) or just paste in the context again.

## Part 2: Getting Set Up (10 minutes)

### Step 2.1 — Create your account
- 2.1.a Go to **claude.ai** in any web browser.
- 2.1.b Click **Sign Up**.
- 2.1.c Enter your email (or sign up with Google) and follow the verification step.
- 2.1.d You now have a free account. No credit card required for the free plan.

### Step 2.2 — Get oriented in the interface
- 2.2.a On the left side, you'll see a sidebar with: **New Chat**, **Chats** (your history), and **Projects**.
- 2.2.b In the middle is the chat window — this is where conversations happen.
- 2.2.c At the bottom is the text box where you type your message. There's also usually a small icon to attach files.

### Step 2.3 — Send your very first message
- 2.3.a Click **New Chat**.
- 2.3.b Type: "Explain what you can help me with, in plain language, for someone who has never used an AI assistant before."
- 2.3.c Press Enter or click the send button.
- 2.3.d Read the response. That's it — you've used Claude. Everything else in this guide builds on this exact action.

## Part 3: Understanding Projects (the single most important feature)

### Step 3.1 — Understand the problem Projects solves
- 3.1.a Without Projects, every new chat forgets everything from your last chat.
- 3.1.b Projects let you create a dedicated space with its own "memory" — instructions that stay active every time you open that Project.
- 3.1.c Think of each Project as hiring a specialist who already knows the rules of their job, forever, without you re-explaining it each time.

### Step 3.2 — Create your first Project
- 3.2.a Click **Projects** in the left sidebar.
- 3.2.b Click **+ New Project**.
- 3.2.c Give it a clear name — name it after the JOB it will do, not a vague label. Example: "Email Replies," not "Project 1."
- 3.2.d Click **Create**.

### Step 3.3 — Add Custom Instructions to your Project
- 3.3.a Inside your new Project, find **Custom Instructions** (sometimes called "Project Instructions" or "Add Instructions").
- 3.3.b Click it. A text box will open.
- 3.3.c Write a clear instruction describing the job. Example: "You help me write short, friendly email replies. Keep them under 100 words unless I say otherwise."
- 3.3.d Click **Save**.

### Step 3.4 — Use your Project
- 3.4.a Open a new chat from inside that Project (not a regular New Chat from the main sidebar — it must be started inside the Project to use its instructions).
- 3.4.b Type your request. Claude will respond using the personality/rules you set up.
- 3.4.c Repeat Steps 3.2–3.4 for every distinct job you want a specialist for. Most regular users end up with somewhere between 3 and 10 active Projects.

## Part 4: Writing Prompts That Actually Work

### Step 4.1 — Understand the #1 mistake beginners make
- 4.1.a Beginners under-explain. They type "write me an email" and get something generic, then conclude "AI isn't that good."
- 4.1.b The fix isn't a magic phrase — it's giving Claude the same context you'd give a new employee on their first day.

### Step 4.2 — Use the 4-part prompt structure
- 4.2.a **Role** (optional but helpful): "You are an expert [whatever]."
- 4.2.b **Task**: exactly what you want done.
- 4.2.c **Context**: the specific details that make it YOUR situation, not a generic one (names, numbers, audience, tone).
- 4.2.d **Format**: how you want the answer structured (bullet points? a table? under 200 words? formal or casual?).

### Step 4.3 — Practice with a real example
- 4.3.a Weak prompt: "Write a follow-up email."
- 4.3.b Strong prompt: "You are a freelance graphic designer. Write a follow-up email to a client named Priya who hasn't responded to a logo proposal I sent 5 days ago. Keep it warm, not pushy, under 100 words, and end with a specific question to make replying easy."
- 4.3.c Notice the strong version gives Role, Task, Context, and an implied Format (length + tone). This is the difference between a generic AI experience and a genuinely useful one.

### Step 4.4 — Iterate instead of starting over
- 4.4.a If the first response isn't right, don't delete it and start a new chat — just respond and say what's wrong.
- 4.4.b Useful iteration phrases: "Make it shorter," "Less formal," "Add more urgency," "Try again but assume the reader knows nothing about this topic."
- 4.4.c Claude keeps the context of the whole conversation, so each correction makes the next answer better — it compounds.

## Part 5: Working With Documents and Files

### Step 5.1 — Upload a file into a conversation
- 5.1.a Look for a paperclip or "+" icon near the message box.
- 5.1.b Click it, select a file from your computer (PDFs, Word docs, spreadsheets, images, and plain text all generally work).
- 5.1.c Once it uploads, type what you want Claude to do with it — "Summarize this," "Find errors in this," "Turn this into a slide outline."

### Step 5.2 — Use Claude for real document work
- 5.2.a **Summarizing**: paste or upload a long document, ask for a summary at a specific length ("3 bullet points," "one paragraph," "explain like I'm not in this industry").
- 5.2.b **Editing**: paste your own writing and ask Claude to tighten it, check tone, or rewrite for a specific audience.
- 5.2.c **Analyzing data**: upload a spreadsheet and ask plain-English questions about it — "What's the trend in column C?" — instead of writing formulas yourself.

### Step 5.3 — Know the honest limits
- 5.3.a Very large files (huge spreadsheets, extremely long documents) may need to be broken into sections.
- 5.3.b Always double-check anything with real consequences (financial figures, legal text, medical information) — Claude is a fast first draft, not a substitute for a professional in those areas.

## Part 6: Artifacts — When Claude Builds Something Separate From the Chat

### Step 6.1 — Understand what an Artifact is
- 6.1.a When you ask Claude to create something substantial (a document, a piece of code, a chart, a webpage), it sometimes opens it in a separate panel called an **Artifact**, instead of just pasting it into the chat.
- 6.1.b This keeps long content out of the conversation flow and lets you see it update live as you ask for changes.

### Step 6.2 — Work with an Artifact
- 6.2.a Once an Artifact appears, you can keep chatting normally — ask for edits, and the Artifact updates in place rather than Claude rewriting the whole conversation.
- 6.2.b There's usually a **copy** or **download** option directly on the Artifact panel.
- 6.2.c If Claude is writing code for you, Artifacts often let you actually preview/run small web pages or visualizations directly.

## Part 7: Putting It Together — A Real Weekly Workflow

### Step 7.1 — Monday: Planning
- 7.1.a Open your "Weekly Planner" Project (or create one using Part 3).
- 7.1.b Paste in everything on your plate. Ask for a prioritized, realistic plan.

### Step 7.2 — Throughout the week: Execution
- 7.2.a Use task-specific Projects (emails, content, research) as things come up — don't try to do everything in one giant chat.
- 7.2.b When a task is unusual or one-off, just use a plain New Chat rather than building a whole Project for it.

### Step 7.3 — Friday: Review
- 7.3.a Ask Claude to help you draft a short weekly recap — what got done, what's carrying over.
- 7.3.b This single habit, done for a month, will teach you more about what Claude is good at for YOUR work than any guide could.

## Part 8: Common Mistakes (and the fast fix for each)

- **Mistake:** Treating every chat as disposable. **Fix:** Build Projects for anything you do more than twice.
- **Mistake:** Vague prompts, then blaming the tool. **Fix:** Use the Role/Task/Context/Format structure from Part 4.
- **Mistake:** Accepting the first draft as final. **Fix:** Iterate — it's faster than starting over, and the model gets more aligned with you each round.
- **Mistake:** Not giving real examples of your own voice/style. **Fix:** Paste a sample of your own past writing and say "match this style" — this single trick improves output quality more than almost anything else.
- **Mistake:** Assuming it knows things it can't know (your business specifics, private data, today's exact news) unless you tell it. **Fix:** Always give it the specific facts it needs — it can't read your mind or your inbox.
